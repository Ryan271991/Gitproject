EFFECTS OF POVERTY

Poverty can mean children going without basics, and it can also mean missing out on everyday fun and activities that other kids take for granted. Poverty harms children's health, social and emotional wellbeing, and education. It harms their childhoods and their futures.

a

Poverty affects all aspects of childhood. It affects friendships and opportunities, creating isolation, stigma and sadness.

Without basic necessities

a

Children who live in poverty are often deprived of opportunities and basic necessities that many others take for granted. It might mean starting the day with an empty stomach because there’s no food in the house, leading to difficulty concentrating and learning in school. Shoes that are too small, worn out, or riddled with holes can make walking uncomfortable and expose children to cold, wet conditions, affecting their health and self-esteem. For some, the idea of a comfortable bed is a distant dream; instead, they sleep on old mattresses placed directly on the floor, which might lack proper insulation or even harbor pests, making it hard to get restful sleep.

Without chances to try new things

a

Children living in poverty have less opportunity to explore their interests and reach their full potential since they are frequently unable to engage in activities that foster their development. Many extracurricular clubs, such as sports teams, music lessons, or art programs, require fees for supplies, equipment, or uniforms—costs that families living in poverty simply cannot afford. Children thus lose out on the opportunity to develop abilities that could influence their future or uncover latent potential.

Growing up too soon

a

For many children, poverty forces them to grow up far too quickly, taking on responsibilities and worries that should not burden them at such a young age. They might have to witness the daily struggles of their parents, such as worrying about paying bills, keeping the lights on, or finding enough food for the family. In some cases, they even feel the pressure to contribute financially by taking on odd jobs or helping out with household chores that go beyond what is typical for their age.
